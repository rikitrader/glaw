# Materiality & the Duty to Disclose

Materiality is the gatekeeper of every disclosure theory: an untrue statement or an omission is
actionable under the securities laws only if it is **material**. This file states the materiality
standard, the special rules for **contingent/speculative** events, the duty-to-disclose / duty-to-
correct/update doctrines, and the **half-truth** principle.

## The materiality standard — the total mix

- ***TSC Industries, Inc. v. Northway, Inc.***, 426 U.S. 438 (1976) — a fact is material if there
  is a **substantial likelihood that a reasonable investor would consider it important** in making
  an investment decision — i.e., that it would have **significantly altered the "total mix"** of
  information available. (Articulated in the proxy context; the standard governs broadly.)
- ***Basic Inc. v. Levinson***, 485 U.S. 224 (1988) — adopted the *TSC* standard for **Rule 10b-5**
  and held that for **contingent or speculative** events (e.g., merger negotiations), materiality is
  a **probability × magnitude** balancing: the probability that the event will occur weighed against
  the anticipated magnitude in light of the total company activity. *Basic* also recognized the
  **fraud-on-the-market** presumption of reliance for efficiently traded securities.
- ***Matrixx Initiatives, Inc. v. Siracusano***, 563 U.S. 27 (2011) — there is **no bright-line
  statistical-significance test**; even data that is not statistically significant can be material
  when the **total mix** would be altered (context-specific, not a numeric threshold).

**Discipline for this seat:** materiality is **argued from the total mix**, never presumed and never
reduced to a single percentage of revenue or income. Quantitative "rules of thumb" (e.g., a 5%
screen) are a **starting point, not a safe harbor** — SEC Staff Accounting Bulletin No. 99 (SAB 99)
warns that a quantitatively small misstatement can be **qualitatively material** (e.g., it turns a
loss into a profit, hits a covenant, or masks a trend).

## The duty to disclose (silence is not always actionable)

There is **no general duty to disclose** every material fact — a duty arises only when:

- A **line-item rule** requires it (e.g., Reg S-K Item 303 MD&A known trends; Item 105 risk
  factors; Item 103 legal proceedings; a specific Form requirement).
- A statute/rule mandates **periodic or current** disclosure (the 8-K event items).
- A **half-truth** has been told — see below.
- A **duty to correct** a prior statement that was false when made, or a **duty to update** a prior
  statement that remains "alive" and has become materially misleading due to subsequent events
  (the existence and scope of a duty to update is debated across circuits — note the uncertainty,
  do not overstate it).
- Insider **trading** while in possession of material non-public information (the "disclose or
  abstain" duty — that is the `/glaw-sec` / insider-trading lens, flagged here).

## The half-truth principle (the most powerful disclosure theory)

A statement that is **literally true but misleading** because it omits a material qualifying fact is
actionable. ***Omnicare, Inc. v. Laborers District Council***, 575 U.S. 175 (2015), addressed
**statements of opinion** under Securities Act § 11: an opinion can be misleading (1) if the
speaker did not actually hold it, or (2) if it **omits material facts about the basis** for the
opinion that conflict with what a reasonable investor would expect. **An accurate statement made
misleading by what it leaves out is as actionable as a lie** — this is the anchor of most omission
findings.

## Forward-looking statements & the bespeaks-caution doctrine

Forward-looking statements (projections, guidance) accompanied by **meaningful cautionary
language** may be protected — the judicial **bespeaks-caution** doctrine and the statutory **PSLRA
safe harbor** (see `non-gaap-forward-looking-and-insider-policy.md`). But the cautionary language
must be **substantive and tailored**, not boilerplate, and must warn of the **risk that actually
materialized** — a "risk" disclosed as hypothetical that had **already occurred** is not protected
and is itself a disclosure defect.

## How this seat applies it

1. For each candidate **statement**: was it **false**, or a **half-truth** made misleading by an
   omission? Was it false **when made** (no hindsight)?
2. For each **omission**: was there a **duty** to disclose (line-item rule, half-truth, correct/
   update)? Was the omitted fact **material** to the **total mix** (qualitative + quantitative, per
   *TSC* / *Basic* / *Matrixx* / SAB 99)?
3. For **contingent** events: apply *Basic*'s **probability × magnitude** balance.
4. **Grade** each finding by **materiality × evidentiary strength**; pin it to the filing/item/line
   and the contradicting contemporaneous fact. An unsupported read is a **lead, not a finding**.
