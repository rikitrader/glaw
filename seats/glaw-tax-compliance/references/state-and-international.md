# State & International (Non-U.S.) Late-Filing Regimes

When the user names a non-IRS jurisdiction, adapt — these regimes are **parallel but
different**. Always verify the specific authority's current rules.

## U.S. state back taxes

- **Separate agency, separate clock.** Each state has its own Department of Revenue / Taxation /
  Franchise Tax Board with its own filing requirements, statutes of limitations, penalty rates,
  and relief programs. Federal relief (FTA, OIC) does **not** bind the state.
- **Sequence after federal.** File federal first so AGI/figures flow into the state return.
- **State look-back / nexus.** Some states pursue more than 6 years, especially for business or
  nexus issues; no SOL on unfiled state returns in many states.
- **Voluntary Disclosure Agreements (VDAs).** Most states offer a VDA that limits the look-back
  period (often 3–4 years) and waives penalties for taxpayers who come forward **before** the
  state contacts them. Strongest tool for unregistered businesses with sales/use or income
  nexus.
- **Tax amnesty.** States periodically run amnesty windows (penalty/interest waivers for a
  fixed period). Check whether one is open or announced.
- **State payment plans / OIC.** Most states have their own installment-agreement and offer
  programs with different formulas than the IRS.

Ask the user the state, then verify that state's: required years, penalty rates, VDA terms,
any open amnesty, and payment-plan/OIC options.

**General state playbook:** if the user came forward **before** the state contacted them and has
nexus/unfiled years (especially a business), the **VDA** is usually the best tool — it caps the
look-back (often 3–4 years) and waives penalties. If the state already assessed or issued a
notice, a VDA may be off the table; pursue the state's payment plan / offer instead. Watch for
**local/municipal** income taxes (OH, MI, PA, NY City, etc.) as separate filings.

## International / U.S. expat & foreign accounts

### Streamlined Filing Compliance Procedures
For taxpayers whose failure to report foreign income/assets was **non-willful**:
- **SFOP — Streamlined Foreign Offshore Procedures**: for those meeting the non-residency
  (physical presence) test. File **Form 14653** (foreign certification). Often **no penalty**.
- **SDOP — Streamlined Domestic Offshore Procedures**: for U.S. residents. File **Form 14654**
  (domestic certification). A reduced **5% miscellaneous offshore penalty** typically applies.
- Both require: amended/delinquent returns for the covered years (generally 3 yrs of returns +
  6 yrs of FBARs) and a **non-willfulness certification** signed under penalty of perjury.

> **Willfulness is the dividing line.** If conduct may have been willful, Streamlined is
> **not** appropriate — route to the **IRS Voluntary Disclosure Practice** through a tax
> attorney *before filing anything* (see persona-and-guardrails.md). A false non-willfulness
> certification is itself a serious problem.

### FBAR — FinCEN Form 114
- Required if aggregate foreign financial accounts exceed **$10,000** at any point in the year.
- Filed **electronically with FinCEN** (not the IRS, not with the 1040), via the BSA E-Filing
  system.
- **Delinquent FBAR Submission Procedures**: if you have no unreported income and weren't
  contacted, you can e-file late FBARs with a reasonable-cause statement, often penalty-free.
- FBAR penalties are severe (non-willful and willful tiers) — accuracy matters.

### FATCA — Form 8938
- Separate from FBAR; filed **with the 1040** when specified foreign financial assets exceed
  thresholds (which vary by filing status and residency). Both FBAR and 8938 can apply to the
  same accounts.

### Other foreign-information returns (high penalties, often abatable for reasonable cause)
- **Form 5471** (foreign corporations), **5472**, **8865** (foreign partnerships), **3520 /
  3520-A** (foreign trusts/gifts), **8621** (PFICs). Late-filing penalties are steep; reasonable
  cause is the usual relief. Flag these when foreign entities are involved.

## Other countries

If the user is dealing with a non-U.S. tax authority (CRA, HMRC, ATO, SAT, SENIAT, etc.),
state that the same *structure* applies — required years, voluntary-disclosure programs,
penalty relief, payment arrangements — but the specifics differ entirely, and recommend a
local tax professional. Adapt the framework; don't apply IRC rules to a foreign system.
