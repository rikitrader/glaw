# Filing Rules: Which Years, Which Deadlines, SFR Replacement

Verify current thresholds per SKILL.md Step 1a (rules and dollar amounts change). Citations
below are to authorities; numbers may need a current check on IRS.gov.

## How many years must actually be filed?

- **The 6-year rule.** IRS Policy Statement 5-133 (IRM 1.2.1.6.18) directs that enforcement of
  delinquency procedures generally requires the **last six years** of returns to bring a
  taxpayer into filing compliance. A manager can require more in egregious cases (business
  filer, large liability, fraud indicators, refund/credit at stake, history of repeated
  delinquency).
- **Assess each case.** Six is the floor for "good standing," not a legal cap. Always file a
  year if it generates a recoverable refund (within RSED) or is needed for a carryforward,
  basis, or downstream-year computation.
- **There is no statute of limitations on an unfiled return.** The IRS can pursue an unfiled
  year indefinitely because the assessment clock never started.

## The three clocks

| Clock | What it limits | When it starts | Typical length |
|---|---|---|---|
| **RSED** (Refund Statute Expiration Date) | Your ability to *get a refund* | Original due date of the return | **3 years** from the time the return is filed, or **2 years** from payment, whichever is later — IRC §6511(a). Withholding and estimated payments are **deemed paid on the original due date** (§6513), and the §6511(b)(2) lookback caps the refund to tax paid in the 3 years before the claim — so for a non-filer the practical rule is "file within **3 years of the original due date** or the refund is lost." |
| **ASED** (Assessment Statute Expiration Date) | IRS's ability to *assess* tax | When the return is **filed** | **3 years** from filing (6 years if >25% income omitted; unlimited for fraud/non-filing) — IRC §6501 |
| **CSED** (Collection Statute Expiration Date) | IRS's ability to *collect* assessed tax | Date of **assessment** | **10 years** — IRC §6502 (see statutes-and-rights.md for tolling) |

### RSED is the urgent one
A refund for a year more than **3 years** past its original due date is **permanently lost** —
the IRS keeps it. When intake shows refund years, check the RSED **first** and prioritize
filing any refund year still inside the window. Conversely, balance-due years have no refund
to lose, so they're sequenced by compliance need, not by a refund clock.

## Substitute for Return (SFR)

- When a taxpayer doesn't file, the IRS may file an **SFR** for them under **IRC §6020(b)**.
- The SFR is built from third-party data (W-2s/1099s) with **no deductions, no dependents, no
  credits, and the worst filing status** — so it almost always **overstates** the liability.
- An SFR **starts the assessment** and therefore the CSED collection clock for that year.
- **Replacing an SFR:** the taxpayer files their **actual original return** for that year
  (Form 1040, *not* 1040-X — there's no valid original return to amend). The IRS processes it
  as the original and typically **reduces** the balance to reflect real deductions/credits.
  - Some IRS units want the return sent to a specific SFR/ASFR address or marked per the SFR
    notice (e.g., CP3219N "90-day letter"). Follow the notice's instructions.
  - If a **CP3219N Notice of Deficiency** was issued, there's a **90-day** window to petition
    Tax Court; filing the real return is the usual response, but mind that deadline.

## Filing order

1. **Oldest required year first, moving forward.** This starts ASED clocks in sequence and
   builds the multi-year compliance record that FTA/IA/OIC require.
2. Use the **correct year's forms and rates** for each year — brackets, standard deduction,
   and credits differ annually.
3. Track per year: refund (claim within RSED) vs. balance due (feeds collection resolution).

## Edge cases

- **Married filers:** a late MFJ election generally can't be made after the deadline if a
  separate return was already filed; check status options per year.
- **Self-employed / Schedule C:** unfiled years also mean **unreported SE tax** and missing
  Social Security credits — reconstructing expenses materially lowers both income tax and SE
  tax.
- **State conformity:** filing federal often triggers a state filing obligation; sequence
  state after federal so AGI flows through. See state-and-international.md.
- **Deceased taxpayer / estate:** final 1040 + possible 1041; different deadlines.
