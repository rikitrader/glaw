# Other Collection Levers & Urgency Flags

Three real items that don't fit the main IA/OIC/CNC flow but materially change strategy. Surface
the relevant one during Step 5 (resolution) or Step 7 (rights/urgency). Verify current dollar
thresholds per SKILL.md Step 1a.

## 1. Passport revocation — §7345 (urgency flag)

- If a taxpayer has **seriously delinquent tax debt** — assessed, legally enforceable federal tax
  debt **over ~$66,000** (2026 figure, **indexed annually**) for which a **lien** has been filed
  (with CDP rights exhausted/lapsed) or a **levy** issued — the IRS **certifies** the debt to the
  State Department, which can **deny, revoke, or limit the passport**.
- Notices: **CP508C** when certified; **CP508R** when the certification is **reversed**.
- Certification is reversed (passport freed) when the debt is paid/falls below the threshold, or
  becomes non-certifiable because the taxpayer enters an **installment agreement**, gets an
  **OIC accepted**, has a **pending CDP hearing**, or has a **pending innocent-spouse** request.
- **Why it matters:** for travelers, dual-nationals, and expats this is often the single biggest
  motivator. Flag it early; entering an IA is usually enough to lift/avoid certification.

## 2. Bankruptcy dischargeability of income tax (alternative resolution)

Some **older income taxes** can be **discharged** in bankruptcy. All of these must be true
(11 U.S.C. §523(a)(1) / §507(a)(8)):

| Rule | Test |
|---|---|
| **3-year rule** | The return was **due (with extensions) more than 3 years** before the bankruptcy petition — §507(a)(8)(A)(i) |
| **2-year rule** | The return was **actually filed more than 2 years** before the petition — §523(a)(1)(B)(ii) |
| **240-day rule** | The tax was **assessed more than 240 days** before the petition — §507(a)(8)(A)(ii) |
| **No fraud / non-evasion** | No fraudulent return and no willful attempt to evade — §523(a)(1)(C) |

**Not dischargeable:** unfiled-year taxes (no return = never dischargeable), trust-fund / payroll
**TFRP** (§6672), recently-assessed or recently-due taxes, and taxes tied to fraud. Tax liens
that already attached to property may survive discharge even when the personal liability is wiped.

**Use:** for a taxpayer with old, properly-filed income-tax debt and no realistic OIC, bankruptcy
can sometimes beat a 10-year CSED wait — but it's a distinct legal process. **Refer to a
bankruptcy attorney**; coordinate timing so the years clear the three rules before filing.

## 3. Innocent spouse vs. injured spouse (joint-liability relief)

Different forms for different problems — don't confuse them:

| Relief | Form | What it does |
|---|---|---|
| **Innocent spouse** — §6015 | **Form 8857** | Relieves you from **joint liability** for tax the *other* spouse understated/underpaid on a **joint return** without your knowledge. Three flavors: innocent-spouse (§6015(b)), separation-of-liability (§6015(c)), and **equitable** relief (§6015(f)). |
| **Injured spouse** | **Form 8379** | You filed a joint return and your share of the **refund** was (or will be) seized to pay the *other* spouse's **separate** pre-existing debt (their back taxes, child support, student loans). Recovers **your** portion of the refund. |

**Flags:** raise innocent-spouse relief whenever one spouse controlled the finances, there was
abuse/coercion, or the liability is entirely the other spouse's. A pending Form 8857 also pauses
collection against the requesting spouse and can reverse a §7345 passport certification.
