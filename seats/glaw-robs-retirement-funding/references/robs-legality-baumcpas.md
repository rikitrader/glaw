# ROBS Legality & The Three Core Compliance Requirements

## Legal foundation (why ROBS is permitted)
ROBS rests on two complementary exemptions that carve it out of the general self-dealing prohibition:
- **IRC §4975(d)(13)** — exempts from the prohibited-transaction excise tax a transaction that is the
  acquisition or sale of **Qualifying Employer Securities (QES)**.
- **ERISA §408(e)** — sets the conditions under which that exemption applies (the QES acquisition rules).
- **ERISA §407(a)** — the qualifying-employer-securities framework these draw on.

The default IRS rule ROBS escapes: a plan holder may not "exchange property between yourself and your
retirement plan," lend money between the parties, furnish services, or "leverage your retirement funds to
your own benefit." ROBS is legal **only** because the QES exemption is satisfied — miss a condition and the
transaction collapses back into a prohibited transaction.

## The three core compliance requirements (the article's spine)

### 1. Adequate consideration (fair-market-value, proportional)
Every leg of the transaction must occur at **fair market value**, and all parties must receive
**proportionally valued** shares. The "Tommy" illustration:
> If the entrepreneur takes shares at **$1 par value** while the retirement plan pays **$10,000 per share**
> for the **same company's** stock, the deal fails — "this transaction was not made in exchange for adequate
> consideration."
Practical hook for the seat: this is **why an independent annual stock valuation is non-negotiable** — the
plan must pay a defensible FMV for the QES, and the founder cannot acquire identical stock at a sweetheart
price. Mispriced share issuance is the single most common adequate-consideration defect.

### 2. No commissions, kickbacks, or finder's fees
No "commission may be charged directly or indirectly to the plan with respect to the transaction." The
entrepreneur may **not personally benefit** from the rollover or the stock issuance. Charging a finder's fee
or pocketing anything off the rollover "violates the tax-exempt status of the rollover."

### 3. Investment limited to QES only
The plan "cannot use funds to acquire investments that are not Qualified Employer Securities." Using rolled
funds to buy non-business assets — residential property, a yacht, any unrelated investment — triggers
disqualification, exposing the participant to "back taxes, penalties, and interest on the funds" plus
"potential criminal charges."

## Consequences of getting it wrong
Loss of the rollover's tax-exempt status → ordinary income tax + penalties + interest on the rolled-over
amount; egregious/abusive structuring carries "potential criminal charges" (specifics not detailed in source).
