# QES Stock Redemption & ROBS Exit / Conversion

> **Source:** Leading Retirement Solutions KB — "Redemption of QES Stock" —
> https://leadingretirement.smartertrack.com/kb/a55/redemption-of-qes-stock.aspx . Ingested 2026-06-20.
> Secondary/practitioner source. Verify against primary law + an ERISA attorney before relying.

## What QES redemption is
A redemption is when **the C-corporation buys some or all of its stock back from the Plan**. It is the normal
mechanism to **exit a ROBS arrangement** or to **convert the C-corp to an S-corp/LLC**. After redemption the
plan holds cash (+ earnings) again instead of company stock.

## The hard rule for conversion (the "and so on" the user asked about)
> **"The C Corporation cannot be converted out of C Corporation status until ALL stock has been redeemed from
> the Plan."**
So the sequencing is mandatory: **full redemption first → then** the S-corp election or LLC conversion. You
cannot flip to S-corp while the plan still owns QES (that also ties back to the Thor trap: staying a C-corp is
foundational *until* you have fully unwound the QES). This is the bridge between "ROBS requires a C-corp" and
"how do I ever become an S-corp" — you redeem the plan out first.

## Valuation on redemption (critical, commonly misunderstood)
> "It is a common misconception that the stock can be bought back for the **original price** that the Plan paid."
Redemption must be at **current fair market value**, not original cost — by an **outside independent firm**
(informal CPA valuation or a formal valuation firm). Valuations are done **annually and/or at significant
events**: initial investment, subsequent investment, **redemption**, or dividend. Each **partial** redemption
generally requires a **fresh valuation**.

## Process steps
1. Corporation engages in the buyback from the Plan.
2. Corporation raises the cash — traditional **bank/SBA loan, retained earnings, or third-party financing**
   (in limited instances the 3rd-party lender can be the individual who runs the company).
3. A **corporate resolution** authorizes the redemption.
4. Proceeds returned to the **Plan account** per its rightful share.
5. Stock **certificates redeemed → become unissued stock**.
6. **Stock ledger** updated to reflect new ownership.
7. Plan funds **reinvested** (e.g., mutual funds) or kept for future business financing.

## Full vs. partial redemption
- **Full:** complete buyback in one transaction.
- **Partial:** smaller amounts bought back over multiple transactions/time; parties may set an **amortized
  payment schedule** to settle the buyout (controlled distribution of profits to the Plan). Partial requires
  **revaluation each time** and extra cost → *"rarely utilized as a result."*

## Post-redemption
Returned monies + earnings can be invested in **traditional investments (mutual funds)** inside the plan, or
redeployed for future non-traditional/business financing.

## Team / compliance
Work with a **Third Party Administrator, an ERISA attorney, and a tax advisor**; draft + execute the corporate
resolution; maintain the stock ledger.

## Net new for the seat (Dossier §8 "exit/unwind")
- **Redeem the plan's QES at current FMV BEFORE any C→S/LLC conversion** — full redemption is a precondition to
  exiting C-corp status. This is the concrete unwind path the seat's risk/exit section should always state.
- Redemption funding sources (bank/SBA, retained earnings, 3rd-party) and the partial-vs-full tradeoff.
