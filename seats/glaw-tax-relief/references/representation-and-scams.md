# DIY vs Representation, and How to Spot a Tax-Relief Scam

The honest guidance an OIC mill won't give. Reuse `persona-and-reality.md` for the framing.

## Do you even need to pay someone?
Many resolutions are **free to apply for yourself**:
- **Short-term plan / streamlined IA** (≤$50k) — set up online in minutes, no representative needed.
- **OIC** — you can file Form 656 yourself; the **$205 fee is waived** for low-income applicants;
  the IRS has a free **OIC Pre-Qualifier** tool.
- **First-Time Abatement** — often one phone call.
- **CNC** — file Form 433-F yourself with documentation.

Pay for help when: the balance is large, a **levy/lien** is active, the financials are complex
(self-employed, assets), an **OIC** needs careful RCP structuring, or you want someone to deal with
the IRS for you. Then the value is **accuracy + representation**, not a magic settlement.

## Who can represent you (Form 2848 Power of Attorney)
- **Enrolled Agent (EA)** — federally licensed, specializes in IRS representation; usually the
  best value for collection cases.
- **CPA** — accounting + representation.
- **Tax attorney** — for liens/levies in litigation, CDP appeals, innocent spouse, or any
  **criminal exposure** (privilege).
Only these three have unlimited practice rights before the IRS.

## How "OIC mills" work (and the IRS warning)
The IRS repeatedly warns that some firms promise to **"settle for pennies on the dollar"** or
**"eliminate your tax debt,"** then:
- Charge **large up-front fees** ($3k–$10k+) before reviewing whether you even qualify.
- Sign everyone up for an **OIC** regardless of RCP — most get **rejected**, fees non-refundable.
- Let **collection deadlines lapse** (the CDP 30-day window) while "working the case."
- Use high-pressure radio/TV/robocall marketing.

## Red flags to tell the user
- "Guaranteed" approval or a specific settlement amount **before** reviewing finances.
- Large fee demanded **up front**, especially non-refundable.
- Claims you "qualify for Fresh Start forgiveness" without asking what you owe, earn, or own.
- Discourages you from contacting the IRS directly.
- Won't put the fee/scope in writing or name the actual EA/CPA/attorney doing the work.

## Honest checklist before hiring anyone
1. Run the **OIC Pre-Qualifier** / the RCP math (`choose-the-path.md`) — know if an OIC is even
   realistic. 2. Confirm the person is an **EA/CPA/attorney** (verifiable). 3. Get **scope + flat
   fee** in writing. 4. Confirm they'll meet the **CDP/levy deadlines**. 5. Keep talking to the
   IRS yourself if you're comfortable — much of this is DIY-able.

> Bottom line: legitimate relief is real and often free to pursue; "pennies on the dollar" for
> everyone is not. This skill gives you the honest path either way.
