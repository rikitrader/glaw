# 09 — Alter Ego / Piercing the Corporate Veil (Florida)

⟦VERIFY all case names/citations and element formulations against current Florida law before filing.⟧

## Threshold rule — NOT an independent cause of action
In Florida, **"alter ego" and "piercing the corporate veil" are equitable remedies, not
free-standing claims.** You must first establish liability on an **underlying substantive
claim** against the entity; veil-piercing then *extends* that liability to the shareholder,
member, manager, parent, or affiliated entity that abused the corporate form.

- **Turner Murphy Co. v. Specialty Constructors, Inc.** — veil piercing is **not** an
  independent cause of action. ⟦VERIFY cite.⟧
- A complaint pleading only "Count: Alter Ego" with no underlying claim is subject to
  dismissal. Plead the substantive count(s), then plead alter ego as a **derivative
  theory of liability / remedy** tied to them.

## Florida alter-ego elements (the three-part test)
To pierce, the plaintiff must plead and prove **all three**:
1. **Complete domination & control** — the entity was a "mere instrumentality" or alter
   ego of the defendant; no separate mind, will, or existence of its own.
2. **Improper / fraudulent purpose** — the corporate form was used to **mislead creditors
   or perpetrate a fraud / injustice** (Florida requires *improper conduct*, not mere
   domination — domination alone is insufficient).
3. **Causation** — that improper use of the form **proximately caused** the plaintiff's
   injury.

### Leading Florida authority
- **Dania Jai-Alai Palace, Inc. v. Sykes** (Fla.) — the foundational veil-piercing
  decision; control alone is not enough, improper/fraudulent use of the entity is

## Facts that support alter-ego allegations (plead the ones in your record)
Assemble every applicable badge; one alone rarely suffices — the **pattern** persuades:
- **Commingling** personal and corporate funds; paying **personal expenses** from company
  accounts.
- **Undercapitalization** at formation or stripping capital later.
- **Failure to observe formalities** — no records, no meetings, no minutes, no separate
  books.
- **Multiple entities used to hide assets**; diverting company revenue to **affiliated
  entities**.
- **Fraudulent transfers / asset stripping** (cross-link → `references/06-fufta`).
- **Shell companies, sham transactions, nominees / straw owners.**
- **Misrepresenting ownership or finances**; using the LLC/corp to **evade creditors**.
- Using the company **to perpetrate the fraud** that is the subject of the underlying
  count.

> Distinguish *fact in the record* from *allegation* from *inference* for each badge
> (SKILL.md guardrail). At the pleading stage you allege; for summary judgment / trial you
> need admissible proof — bank records, ledgers, UCC filings, formation docs, tax returns,
> deposition admissions.

## Related / sibling liability-extension theories
When the facts don't fit classic veil-piercing, consider these (each has its own
elements — ⟦VERIFY⟧):
- **Single Business Enterprise / "mere continuation"** theory — multiple entities operated
  as one; Florida courts are cautious about this label, often folding it back into the
  three-part alter-ego test.
- **Successor liability** — express/implied assumption, de facto merger, mere
  continuation, or fraudulent transaction to escape liability.
- **Joint venture / agency / employer (respondeat superior) liability** — direct, not
  derivative; connect the individual through control and benefit.
- **Aiding & abetting** breach of fiduciary duty; **civil conspiracy** (needs an
  underlying tort + agreement).
- **FUFTA** (Ch. 726) — when value left the entity to an insider; the *fastest* route to
  reach diverted assets without proving full domination.

## Typical pleading architecture (underlying claim + remedy)
A Florida complaint commonly stacks substantive counts, then the extension theories:
```
Count I    – Breach of Contract                          (entity)
Count II   – Fraud / Fraudulent Inducement               (entity + individual)
Count III  – Civil Theft (§772.11; pre-suit demand!)     (entity + individual)
Count IV   – Fraudulent Transfer (FUFTA, Ch. 726)        (transferor + transferee)
Count V    – Civil Conspiracy                            (all who agreed)
Count VI   – Alter Ego / Piercing the Corporate Veil     (owner) — REMEDY, ties to I–V
Count VII  – Successor Liability                         (successor entity)
Count VIII – Declaratory Judgment                        (status/relationship)
```
Other substantive counts that frequently anchor a veil-piercing remedy: negligent
misrepresentation, constructive fraud, conversion, unjust enrichment, breach of fiduciary
duty, promissory estoppel, quantum meruit, account stated, open account, tortious
interference, FDUTPA (§501.201 et seq.), unpaid-wage claims, construction-lien/contractor
claims (Ch. 713), professional negligence, securities fraud, and state/federal RICO.

> **Civil theft trap:** §772.11 requires a **written pre-suit demand** and proof of
> criminal-theft intent by **clear and convincing evidence**; treble damages + fees follow,
> but a missed demand sinks the count. ⟦VERIFY current statute.⟧

## Build order
1. **Lock the underlying claim** against the entity (≥1 substantive count that survives a
   motion to dismiss). Without it, alter ego has nothing to attach to.
2. **Map control** — who owns/runs the entity; signatory authority; who took the money.
3. **Assemble the badges** above from the record; tie each to a document/§.
4. **Prove improper use + causation** — connect the abuse of form to the plaintiff's
   specific loss.
5. **Plead alter ego as a remedy count** referencing the substantive counts; add successor
   / FUFTA / conspiracy where the facts fit.
6. **Pierce procedurally** — name the individuals/affiliates as defendants; consider an
   injunction/receiver to freeze diverted assets; calendar all limitations periods.

## Discovery to "connect" an individual to the entity
To establish domination, demand: formation & ownership docs, operating/shareholder
agreements, cap tables, bank statements and check images (commingling), QuickBooks/ledger
exports, intercompany transfers, payroll/distribution records, tax returns (entity +
K-1s), leases and credit applications signed personally, UCC-1 financing statements,
Sunbiz/Secretary-of-State filings and registered-agent changes, and the individual's
control over other affiliated entities. Depose the bookkeeper, the registered agent, and
the principal. Cross-link → `references/07-document-forensics-lost-note.md` for
authenticity and `references/06-fufta-fraudulent-transfer.md` for the asset trail.
