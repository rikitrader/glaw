# 04 — MCA, Usury & True-Lender

⟦VERIFY citations; usury is intensely state-specific and fact-specific.⟧

## Is it a loan or a purchase? (recharacterization)
A **merchant cash advance (MCA)** is structured as a *purchase of future receivables*,
not a loan, to avoid usury and lender-licensing law. Courts test substance over form.
Common multi-factor test (e.g., NY's *LG Funding v. United Senior Properties*) asks:
1. **Reconciliation** — can the merchant adjust payments to actual receivables?
2. **Finite term / absolute repayment** — is there a fixed term and an unconditional
   obligation to repay regardless of business performance?
3. **Recourse on default / who bears the risk** — does the funder bear the risk of the
   merchant's failure, or is it effectively guaranteed?
If repayment is absolute, term is fixed, and the funder bears no real performance risk
→ it looks like a **loan**, and usury/licensing law may apply.

**Red flag pattern:** an MCA that *defaults* and is then **"converted" into a term
loan** for the full claimed balance. That balance typically bakes in the MCA's factor/
fees; relabeling it "loan principal" that then accrues further interest can stack
charges and invites a usury / unconscionability / recharacterization attack. Demand:
the original advance amount (cash actually funded), the factor rate, all payments, and
a reconciliation ledger.

## Usury — the maps
- **Florida** (Ch. 687): civil usury generally **>18%** on loans of $500,000 or less
  (and >25% on larger) ⟦VERIFY thresholds⟧; **criminal usury** §687.071: **>25%**
  (and **>45%** = felony loan-sharking). Remedies can include forfeiture of interest
  and, for criminal-rate loans, potential unenforceability. Corporate/large-loan
  exemptions exist.
- **California** (Cal. Const. art. XV): general cap **~10%** for non-exempt lenders;
  many licensed-lender exemptions. A 16%+ note governed by CA law for a non-exempt
  lender is exposed.
- **New York**: civil usury 16%, criminal 25% ⟦VERIFY⟧ — but corporate borrowers
  often can't raise *civil* usury, only criminal-rate; relevant if NY forum/law.
- **Choice of law**: a note may pick a permissive state's law; courts may still apply
  the protective state's usury law on public-policy grounds, especially for
  consumer-ish or in-state borrowers. Identify borrower location and where performance
  occurs.

## Practice points
- Separate the **stated rate**, **default rate**, **late/per-diem charges**, and
  **fees** — aggregate them; per-diem "accrual" can push the effective rate over the
  cap.
- A genuine **usury savings clause** helps the lender but does not always cure a
  knowing criminal-rate charge.
- For the borrower: usury is both a *defense* and (sometimes) an affirmative claim.
