# 07 — Document Forensics & Lost-Instrument Proof

## Authenticity review (do this on every instrument)
- **Execution**: Is every required party's signature present? Any **blank signature
  block** (esp. the *lender/plaintiff* on its own agreement)? Missing dates?
- **Capacity**: Did the signer sign in the capacity the document requires (individual
  guarantor vs. entity officer)? Mismatch can void the obligation against that party.
- **Internal consistency**: Defined terms used but never defined; references to
  exhibits/schedules that are **blank**; a recital calling the document an
  "Amendment" when it is a stand-alone agreement; "the Corporation" used for an LLC.
- **Date integrity**: Effective date vs. signature dates vs. certificate dates vs.
  notary dates — list them; certificates that must be "dated the date of closing"
  bearing several different dates is a defect.
- **Sworn-but-absent attachments**: A certificate swearing "attached hereto as
  Exhibit __ are true and correct copies of [resolutions/articles/good-standing/debt
  schedule]" with a **blank** exhibit page = a **false certification**.
- **Cloned signatures**: The *identical* signature image reused across many entities/
  pages is normal for one signer but, combined with blank dates and blank exhibits,
  is a forensic flag worth preserving (and a basis to demand originals/metadata).
- **Amount integrity**: Face amount vs. amount advanced vs. claimed vs. adjudged.
  Demand the ledger; a note that says the balance "is not necessarily the face amount"
  but is enforced at face, with no accounting, is suspect.

## Suspected alteration / forgery
- Compare every copy of the "same" instrument across sources (closing set vs. court
  exhibit vs. recorded copy) **clause by clause**; identical OCR-level typos confirm a
  common source, while *differences* in amount/terms/signatures between supposedly
  identical instruments are the alteration signal.
- Preserve: native files + metadata, all versions, custody chain. Consider a
  questioned-document examiner for handwriting/ink/printing where forgery is alleged.

## Lost-instrument reestablishment (Fla. Stat. §673.3091) — attack/defend
- Proponent must prove entitlement to enforce when loss occurred, loss not by
  transfer/seizure, and inability to reasonably obtain possession; court must order
  **adequate protection** for the obligor against double liability.
- **Attack angles**: proponent actually possesses the original (or a full executed
  copy in a closing binder it controls); "loss" pleaded to dodge production/
  authentication; no real risk analysis for the obligor; the "lost note" count paired
  with a *breach* count on the same note is internally inconsistent.
- **Defend angles**: statute expressly permits reestablishment; attaching a copy is
  the proper way to prove terms; adequate-protection language satisfies the statute.
