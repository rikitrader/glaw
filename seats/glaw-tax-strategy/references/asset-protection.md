# Asset Protection — shielding wealth from lawsuits, judgments & creditors

Wealth preservation runs alongside tax planning: the goal is to make assets hard to reach if
you're sued, **without** committing fraudulent transfer. Verify current state caps per Step 1a;
asset-protection law is highly **state-specific** — involve a local attorney.

## The cardinal rule: protect EARLY, never after a claim

- **Fraudulent transfer (UVTA / FUFTA)** — moving assets to "hinder, delay, or defraud" a creditor
  is **voidable**, and doing it after a claim arises (or when insolvent) can be unwound and is a
  **badge of fraud**. Asset protection is a **before-the-storm** exercise — set it up when no claim
  is pending or foreseeable. Doing it mid-lawsuit is worse than useless.
- We protect against *future, unknown* creditors — never to escape an existing/known one.

## Layer 1 — insurance (always first, cheapest)
Adequate **liability + umbrella** coverage and, for businesses, **D&O / E&O / professional
liability**. Insurance pays the claim so the structure never gets tested. No structure replaces
coverage.

## Layer 2 — entity segregation (the corporate veil)
- Operate every risky activity inside an **LLC or corporation**; never personally. Keeps a
  business lawsuit from reaching personal assets (and vice-versa).
- **Separate entity per risk silo** (each rental property, each operating line) — a "**series
  LLC**" (allowed in some states) or multiple LLCs isolate one asset's liability from another.
- **Holdco/Opco** — operating risk in opcos, valuable assets (IP, cash, real estate) in a holding
  entity a plaintiff can't reach.
- **Maintain the veil**: separate bank accounts, no commingling, real capitalization, minutes,
  arm's-length intercompany terms — or a creditor pierces it (alter-ego; cross-ref
  `elite-corporate-counsel`).

## Layer 3 — statutory exemptions (free protection by operation of law)
- **ERISA-qualified plans** (401(k), pension) — generally **unlimited** creditor protection;
  anti-alienation (§206(d)), *Patterson v. Shumate*.
- **IRAs** — protected in bankruptcy up to an inflation-indexed cap (~$1.7M range; **rollover**
  IRAs from ERISA plans are **fully** protected); **inherited IRAs are NOT protected**
  (*Clark v. Rameker*, 2014). State protection outside bankruptcy varies.
- **Homestead** — **Florida & Texas** protect unlimited home equity (acreage limits); most states
  cap it. BAPCPA limits homestead acquired <1,215 days before bankruptcy (~$190k+ — verify).
- **Tenancy by the entirety** — in many states, property held by a married couple is shielded from
  one spouse's individual creditors.
- **Life insurance & annuity** cash value — protected in many states (e.g., FL).

## Layer 4 — protective trusts (irreversible, strongest; see trusts reference)
- **Domestic Asset Protection Trust (DAPT)** — a self-settled spendthrift trust in a DAPT state
  (NV, SD, AK, DE, WY, OH, TN, and others). You can be a discretionary beneficiary yet shield
  assets from future creditors after the state's seasoning period. Effectiveness against
  out-of-state creditors is litigated — strongest if you reside in the DAPT state.
- **Offshore APT** (Cook Islands, Nevis) — the gold standard for deterrence (foreign courts
  ignore US judgments; short fraudulent-transfer statutes), but costly, reporting-heavy
  (**FBAR/FATCA**, must be **tax-neutral** — a grantor trust, no tax savings), and IRS-scrutinized.
  Deterrence, not tax avoidance.
- **Irrevocable trusts generally** — once assets are truly given up (irrevocable, not self-settled
  in non-DAPT states), they're outside your estate **and** outside creditors' reach.

## Layer 5 — equity stripping
Encumber otherwise-exposed equity (e.g., a friendly LOC/mortgage on real estate, or a security
interest to a holdco) so there's little net equity for a creditor to seize. Must be a **bona fide**
debt, not a sham.

## Coordinating with tax
- Most asset protection is **tax-neutral** by design (DAPTs/offshore APTs are usually grantor
  trusts — no income-tax change). Don't expect protection structures to also save income tax;
  conflating the two is a red flag.
- The big **overlap** wins: **retirement accounts** (protected *and* tax-advantaged) and
  **irrevocable gift trusts** (estate-tax *and* creditor benefits) — prioritize these.

## Hard lines
- **No fraudulent transfers, no hiding assets, no sham debt.** That's not protection — it's
  voidable (and can be criminal). For anything adversarial or near a claim, route to an
  asset-protection attorney **and** `elite-corporate-counsel`; this skill is informational only.
