# Settlement, Stipulation & Trial — prosecuting the docketed case

Most docketed Tax Court cases settle. The work is therefore (1) the stipulation of facts, (2) the
hazards-of-litigation settlement with IRS Counsel, and only then (3) trial. Run each through the
seat's order of operations.

## The stipulation of facts (Rule 91) — the battlefield

Tax Court Rule 91 **requires** the parties to stipulate, to the fullest extent possible, all facts,
documents, and evidence not genuinely in dispute. Practical consequences:

- **Stipulate everything you can prove and nothing you cannot.** A fact stipulated is established;
  a fact you wrongly stipulate is gone forever. Tie every proposed stipulation to the audit record
  / the GL-tied substantiation index — never stipulate to a number you have not verified.
- **Authentication & admissibility** are handled in the stip — stipulated documents come in without
  a foundation witness, which is most of what a fact-bound substantiation case needs.
- **Rule 91(f)** lets a party move to compel stipulation when the other side stonewalls; the court
  can deem matters stipulated. Use it; the IRS uses it.
- The stipulation **frames the trial** — only the genuinely disputed facts go to evidence, which is
  why winning the stip wins the case.

## Branerton informal discovery (Rule 70 predicate)

The Tax Court expects parties to **try informal discovery first** (*Branerton Corp. v.
Commissioner*, 61 T.C. 691 (1974)) before serving formal interrogatories/document requests. A
Branerton letter to IRS Counsel opens the exchange and builds the stipulation. Formal discovery
(Rules 70–76) is available but narrower than district court — depositions generally require leave.

## Settlement on hazards of litigation

IRS Area/Field Counsel and Appeals settle on **hazards** — the probability that the IRS's position
fails at trial, applied to the dollars at stake — not on sympathy or "splitting the difference."

To build a credible settlement posture:

1. **Identify each issue and the IRS's primary and alternative theories** (from the §6512 scope).
2. **Assess the litigating hazard per issue** — strength of the substantiation, controlling
   authority (under the *Golsen* circuit), and how distinguishable the IRS's cases are. Route the
   case-law strength assessment to `/glaw-case-law-research`.
3. **Quantify** — probability-weight each issue to a settlement range. Route the dollar math to
   `/glaw-tax-strategy` / `/glaw-tax-provision`; valuation/damages to `/glaw-financial-forensics`.
4. **Basis-of-settlement memo** — document each concession and the hazards rationale, so the file
   shows the settlement was reasoned, not surrendered.

A **qualified offer** (§7430(g)) made early can shift the IRS's incentives and preserve a fee claim
if the case goes to judgment at or below the offer.

## Trial (regular case)

- **Standing Pretrial Order** sets the deadlines: stipulation, pretrial memoranda, exchange of
  exhibits and witness lists. Miss them and exhibits/witnesses can be excluded.
- **Trial** is to a single judge, no jury, often on a city trial-session calendar. The Federal
  Rules of Evidence apply in regular cases (Rule 143); they are relaxed in S cases.
- **Expert witnesses (Rule 143(g))** submit a **written report that serves as direct testimony** —
  the report must be lodged before trial. Route expert reports (valuation, reasonable comp,
  economic substance) to `/glaw-financial-forensics` / `/glaw-expert-witness`; an inadequate or
  late report gets the expert excluded.
- **Post-trial briefs (Rule 151)** — usually simultaneous opening + answering; this is where the
  legal argument is actually made to the judge.

## Opinion, decision, and finality

- The court issues an **opinion** (T.C., memorandum, or — in S cases — a non-precedential Summary
  Opinion), then a **decision** (the dollar judgment).
- **Rule 155 computation:** if the opinion decides principles but leaves the numbers, the parties
  compute the deficiency under the opinion and submit agreed (or competing) computations.
- **Finality (§7481):** the decision becomes final 90 days after entry unless a **notice of appeal**
  is filed (§7483). S-case decisions are **final and unappealable** (§7463(b)).
- **Appeal** (regular cases) goes to the regional U.S. Court of Appeals (§7482); coordinate with
  `/glaw-appellate` and `/glaw-federal-trial-counsel`.

## ⛔ Adversarial gate before filing or settling

No petition is filed and no stipulated decision is signed until `/glaw-adversarial` runs the **IRS
Chief Counsel** RED→BLUE against it — attacking jurisdiction (valid notice? timely petition?), the
substantiation, the legal theory under the *Golsen* circuit, and the settlement range. A position
Counsel would defeat is reworked, not filed. Record sign-off via `/glaw-chief-decision`.
