# Small-Tax-Case (S) Election & Case Procedure

## The §7463 small-tax-case election — a deliberate choice, not a default

A taxpayer may elect to have the case conducted as a **small tax case (S case)** under IRC §7463
if the amount in dispute is **$50,000 or less per year** (the disputed deficiency *and* any claimed
overpayment, year by year — verify the current ceiling in `tax-legal-shared/current-figures.md`).

| | **S case (§7463)** | **Regular case** |
|---|---|---|
| Speed / formality | Faster, informal; relaxed evidence rules | Full procedure; Federal Rules of Evidence apply (Rule 143) |
| Precedent | **No precedent** (T.C. Summary Opinion) | Precedential (T.C. or memorandum opinion) |
| **Appeal** | **None — the decision is final and unappealable** (§7463(b)) | Appealable to the Court of Appeals (§7482) |
| Best for | Small, fact-bound disputes where finality and cost beat the right to appeal | Larger stakes, legal issues, or a position worth preserving for appeal |

**Decision rule:** elect S only when (a) every year is at or under the ceiling, (b) the dispute is
fact-bound (substantiation), and (c) the client values speed and low cost over the right to appeal.
If a genuine legal issue is in play or the stakes justify appellate review, **do not elect S**.
The election can be made in the petition and can be removed before trial on motion (Rule 171), but
treat it as consequential. **AskUserQuestion on the speed-vs-appeal tradeoff before electing.**

## Burden of proof (who has to prove what)

- **Default:** the Commissioner's determination is presumed correct; the **taxpayer bears the
  burden** of proving it wrong (Tax Court Rule 142(a); *Welch v. Helvering*, 290 U.S. 111 (1933)).
- **§7491 shift:** the burden can shift to the IRS on a factual issue if the taxpayer introduces
  **credible evidence**, has **complied with substantiation and recordkeeping** requirements, and
  **cooperated** with reasonable IRS requests. It is narrower than clients expect — never plan a
  case around it.
- **Fraud (§6663 / §6651(f)):** the IRS must prove fraud by **clear and convincing evidence**.
- **New matter / increased deficiency:** burden is on the **Commissioner** (Rule 142(a)).
- **Unreported-income cases:** the IRS generally must link the taxpayer to an income-producing
  activity before the presumption attaches (the *Weimerskirch* line) — a real attack point.

## The case timeline (regular case)

1. **Petition filed** (within the jurisdictional window) → IRS **answers** (Rule 36, ~60 days).
2. **Branerton informal discovery** — the Tax Court *requires* parties to attempt informal
   exchange before formal discovery (*Branerton Corp. v. Commissioner*, 61 T.C. 691 (1974)).
3. **Stipulation of facts (Rule 91)** — the parties are **required** to stipulate all facts and
   documents not genuinely in dispute. This is where most of the case is actually won or lost.
4. **Formal discovery** (Rules 70–76) — interrogatories, document requests, depositions (rare;
   leave of court usually needed). Discovery is narrower than district court.
5. **Trial-readiness:** Standing Pretrial Order, pretrial memoranda, exhibits, witness lists.
6. **Calendar call → trial** before a single judge (no jury). Post-trial briefs (Rule 151).
7. **Opinion → decision** (Rule 155 computation if numbers must be recomputed). Decision becomes
   **final** 90 days after entry unless a notice of appeal is filed (§7481/§7483).

## Settlement track (where ~90% of docketed cases go)

- **Docketed-case Appeals referral:** a case that was **not** previously before IRS Appeals is
  generally referred for a settlement conference after the petition is filed. Pursue it.
- **IRS Area Counsel** negotiates on **hazards of litigation** — the probability-weighted value of
  the litigating positions, not a "split the baby." Document each concession and its hazards basis.
- **Stipulated decision:** a settled case ends in a stipulated decision document the court enters.
  Run it past the adversarial gate (IRS Chief Counsel RED→BLUE) before signing.

## Costs and fees

- **§7430** allows recovery of reasonable litigation/administrative costs if the taxpayer
  **substantially prevails** and the IRS's position was **not substantially justified** — but the
  taxpayer must have **exhausted administrative remedies** and menet the net-worth limits. Rarely
  awarded; do not promise it.
- A **qualified offer** under §7430(g) can shift fees if the IRS rejects it and the judgment is
  at or below the offer — a real tactical tool in the right case.
