# Scams, Impersonation & Data Security

An audit-defense seat must distinguish a **real IRS contact** from an **impersonation scam**, and
must protect the taxpayer's and the preparer's data — because identity theft and stolen client
data routinely **trigger** the fraudulent-return / mismatch notices that look like exam problems.

> Source spine: Tax Tips 2018-44 (phone scam intensifies), 2018-32 (refund scam), 2018-92
> (disaster scams), 2023-44 (Employee Retention Credit schemes), 2021-07 (IP PINs now open to all
> taxpayers), 2026-44 (IRS-verified social media), 2026-37 (Small Business Week: avoid the scam),
> and the preparer data-security series 2018-23 / 2018-137 (spearphishing) / 2018-151 (data
> security plan) / 2018-160 (after a data theft). Authority: IRS Pub 4557, Pub 5293, FTC
> Safeguards Rule, IRC §6713 / §7216 (disclosure of return info).

## The cardinal authentication rule — "letter ≠ phone call"

**The IRS's first contact is by MAIL.** The IRS will **not**:
- call to **demand immediate payment**, especially by **gift card, wire, or cryptocurrency**;
- **threaten** to bring in police/immigration to **arrest** the taxpayer for non-payment;
- demand payment **without** the opportunity to question or appeal the amount;
- ask for **card numbers** over the phone or text.

Any contact that does these things is a **scam** (Tax Tip 2018-44). A real balance-due always
arrives first as a **CP/Letter** verifiable on IRS.gov and in the taxpayer's **online account**.
Tell anxious clients this explicitly before they react to a "call from the IRS."

**Verify the IRS, not just the message:** the IRS's authentic outreach on social platforms and
e-News carries an **"IRS-verified"** marker (Tax Tip 2026-44) — direct clients to IRS.gov and the
verified accounts, not to links in unsolicited messages.

## Common scams to recognize

- **Phone-impersonation / "you owe, pay now"** (2018-44) — robocalls or live callers demanding
  immediate payment with threats. Report to **TIGTA** (Treasury Inspector General for Tax
  Administration) and the FTC.
- **Refund / "extra refund" scams** (2018-32) — messages claiming a surprise refund to harvest
  bank/SSN data, or fraudsters depositing a **bogus refund** into the victim's account then
  "recalling" it (return the erroneous refund per IRS procedure; don't spend it).
- **Disaster scams** (2018-92) — fake charities and fake "IRS disaster relief" after FEMA events.
- **Phishing / smishing / spearphishing** (2018-137) — emails/texts impersonating the IRS, a
  software vendor, or a client to steal credentials. The IRS does not initiate contact by email
  or text to request personal or financial information.

## Employee Retention Credit (ERC) promoter schemes  — *(Tax Tip 2023-44)*

Aggressive promoters pushed businesses to claim the **Employee Retention Credit** they did not
qualify for, for a contingency fee. This is now a live audit/enforcement area. If a client has an
ERC exposure:

- **Withdrawal program** — a business that has filed an ERC claim **not yet paid (or paid but not
  cashed)** can **withdraw** the claim so it is treated as if never filed, avoiding penalties and
  interest on that claim.
- **Voluntary Disclosure Program (ERC-VDP)** — for claims **already received**, the IRS has run
  VDP windows letting a business repay a discounted portion and avoid penalties/interest.
- Eligibility is **narrow** (government shutdown order or a significant decline in gross receipts
  in the relevant quarters). Recompute eligibility from the books before defending the claim; if
  it doesn't qualify, route to the withdrawal/VDP path — never manufacture eligibility.

Watch the deadlines and current program status — defer to `current-figures.md` and verify on
IRS.gov; route the criminal-exposure question to `/glaw-investigations`.

## Identity Protection PIN (IP PIN)  — *(Tax Tip 2021-07)*

The **IP PIN** is a six-digit number that blocks anyone else from e-filing a return with the
taxpayer's SSN. **All taxpayers are now eligible** (not just confirmed ID-theft victims) — opt in
through the IRS online account. For a client whose exam stems from a **fraudulently filed return
or SSN mismatch**, an IP PIN is part of the cleanup, alongside **Form 14039** below.

## If the taxpayer is an identity-theft victim

1. **Form 14039 — Identity Theft Affidavit** to the IRS.
2. Report to the **FTC** (IdentityTheft.gov) and place fraud alerts/credit freezes.
3. Respond to the IRS notice flagging the fraudulent return; the IRS works it through its Identity
   Theft Victim Assistance process.
4. Enroll in an **IP PIN**.

## Preparer / firm data-security duty  — *(Tax Tips 2018-151, 2018-23, 2018-137, 2018-160)*

Tax professionals are **legally required** to have a **written information-security plan (WISP)**
— mandated by the **FTC Safeguards Rule** (Gramm-Leach-Bliley) and detailed in **IRS Pub 4557
(Safeguarding Taxpayer Data)** and **Pub 5293**. The "Security Six" baseline: anti-virus,
firewalls, two-factor authentication, backup, drive encryption, and a VPN. Unauthorized
disclosure of return information carries **§6713 / §7216** exposure.

### After a data theft — the response steps (Tax Tip 2018-160)

1. **Report to the IRS** — contact your **IRS Stakeholder Liaison** immediately; they alert IRS
   Criminal Investigation and help protect affected clients' accounts.
2. **Report to the FBI** and local police if directed; report to the **state** (the state attorney
   general and/or the state tax agency where clients reside).
3. **Notify clients** so they can file **Form 14039** and obtain IP PINs.
4. **Engage a security professional** to determine the breach scope and remediate.
5. **Report to the FTC** and to the appropriate insurer.

Keep this distinct from the audit work: a data breach can be **why** a client's return was filed
fraudulently and is now under scrutiny — handle the security incident **and** the resulting
notice (using `notices-and-letters.md`).

## What you do with all this in an exam

- **Authenticate the notice first** (letter ≠ phone call) before the client pays or panics.
- If the underlying problem is **identity theft**, the defense is Form 14039 + IP PIN + the
  fraudulent-return cleanup, **not** a substantiation fight over income the taxpayer never earned.
- If it's an **ERC** matter, recompute eligibility and route to withdrawal/VDP if it fails.
- If it's a **preparer breach**, run the data-theft response steps in parallel with the notice.

`tax-legal-shared/current-figures.md` — verify current on IRS.gov.
