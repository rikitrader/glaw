# Remedies, Cooperation Credit & Parallel Proceedings

A Wells strategy is incomplete without a clear-eyed view of **what the SEC can take**, how
**cooperation** can reduce it, and how **parallel** criminal/regulatory tracks constrain everything
you say. This file maps the remedies, the **Seaboard** cooperation framework, and the parallel-
proceedings discipline.

## Remedies the SEC can seek

Depending on the violation, the respondent, and the forum, the Commission can pursue:

- **Civil monetary penalties** — tiered by the severity of the violation and whether the respondent
  is an individual or an entity; the tier amounts are inflation-adjusted (quote current amounts from
  `tax-legal-shared/current-figures.md`).
- **Disgorgement** of ill-gotten gains, plus **prejudgment interest** — the SEC's authority and its
  limits were shaped by ***Kokesh v. SEC***, 581 U.S. 455 (2017) (disgorgement is a "penalty" for
  statute-of-limitations purposes) and ***Liu v. SEC***, 591 U.S. 71 (2020) (disgorgement in a
  district-court action is permissible as **equitable relief** limited to **net profits** and
  generally **returned to investors**); statutory amendments (the NDAA 2021 amendments to the
  Exchange Act) addressed the SEC's disgorgement authority and its limitations period — **verify the
  current statutory framework.**
- **Injunctions** — orders against future violations (obey-the-law and conduct-based).
- **Officer-and-director (O&D) bars** — barring an individual from serving as an officer or director
  of a public company.
- **Industry bars / suspensions** — barring association with regulated entities (broker-dealers,
  investment advisers) — the **collateral** consequence that often matters most to an individual.
- **Cease-and-desist** orders (administrative).
- **Trading suspensions** and, for issuers, **deregistration** in extreme cases.
- **Penny-stock bars**, **rule 102(e)** practice bars (for accountants/attorneys appearing before
  the Commission), and **whistleblower-related** consequences.

The remedies argument in a Wells submission targets **narrowing** these — no penalty or a lower
tier, no bar, disgorgement limited to net profits returned to investors, no admissions.

## Cooperation credit — the Seaboard factors

In the **Seaboard Report** (*Report of Investigation Pursuant to Section 21(a)*, Exchange Act
Release No. 44969 (2001)), the Commission set out the framework for crediting a company's
**self-policing, self-reporting, remediation, and cooperation**. The four broad categories
(elaborated by 13 detailed questions):

1. **Self-policing** — the strength of the company's compliance culture and controls **before** the
   misconduct.
2. **Self-reporting** — whether the company **discovered and disclosed** the conduct promptly to the
   Commission (and to the public/regulators).
3. **Remediation** — what the company did to **fix** the problem: discipline, restitution,
   strengthened controls, new compliance.
4. **Cooperation** — the extent and quality of cooperation with the staff's investigation
   (documents, access, candor).

The SEC's **cooperation tools** for **individuals** include **cooperation agreements**, **deferred-
prosecution agreements (DPAs)**, **non-prosecution agreements (NPAs)**, and proffer/immunity
arrangements (coordinated with the cooperation program). Building a documented, **truthful**
Seaboard / cooperation narrative is often the highest-value part of a Wells strategy — but it must
rest on **real** remediation, not a manufactured one.

## Settlement posture

- Most matters **settle**; settlements historically proceed **"neither admit nor deny,"** though the
  Commission requires **admissions** in certain categories of cases.
- A settlement is documented via an **Offer of Settlement** and the Commission's order or a consent
  judgment; **collateral consequences** (bars, statutory disqualifications, "bad actor"
  disqualification under Rule 506(d), waivers) must be mapped **before** agreeing — a settlement that
  triggers an automatic disqualification can be worse than the headline remedy.

## Parallel proceedings (the constraint on everything)

- **Criminal (DOJ).** The most serious parallel track. SEC testimony and any **Wells submission**
  can be used by prosecutors; Form 1662 warns of this. **Coordinate with criminal-defense counsel
  before any submission**; for individuals, weigh the **Fifth Amendment** (an adverse inference is
  permitted in a **civil** case but the protection against criminal use is paramount). Route to
  `/glaw-investigations` / `/glaw-federal-trial-counsel`.
- **SROs (FINRA) and state regulators** — separate actions; coordinate the narrative.
- **Private securities litigation** — a Wells submission may be **discoverable** to private
  plaintiffs; assume it will be read by the class-action bar.
