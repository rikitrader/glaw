# The Wells Process & the Submission — Strategy and Architecture

The **Wells process** is the opportunity the SEC enforcement staff affords a prospective respondent
to argue, **before** the staff finalizes its recommendation, why an enforcement action should not be
brought (or should be narrowed). This file covers the origin and nature of the process, the
**make / don't-make** decision, and how to architect a submission that persuades without doing harm.

## Origin & nature

- The process is named for the **Wells Committee** (the Advisory Committee on Enforcement Policies
  and Practices), and was formalized in **Securities Act Release No. 5310** (1972), which announced
  the policy of giving prospective defendants/respondents an opportunity to submit a written
  statement to the staff. The procedure is reflected in the SEC's **Rules Relating to Investigations
  / Rules on Informal and Other Procedures** (commonly cited as **17 C.F.R. § 202.5(c)**).
- It is a matter of **staff practice and policy**, **not** an absolute legal right — the staff may,
  and usually does, give a Wells notice, but exceptions exist (e.g., emergency actions, certain
  follow-on matters). The current procedures and any submission-format limits are described in the
  SEC **Enforcement Manual** — verify them.
- A Wells submission is **voluntary advocacy**. It is generally **admissible against the submitter**
  in the SEC's action and may be **shared with other authorities** (including criminal prosecutors)
  — the controlling downside that shapes every decision below.

## The make / don't-make decision (the default is caution)

Do **not** assume a submission should be made. Weigh, in writing:

### Reasons to submit
- **Persuade the staff** to recommend no action, or a **narrower** theory / fewer charges /
  lower remedies.
- **Reach the Commission** — the submission travels with the staff's recommendation, so it can
  persuade the **Commissioners** even if the staff is set.
- **Open settlement** — frame a realistic resolution and signal cooperation.
- **Correct the record** — fix a factual misunderstanding the staff is operating under.

### Reasons NOT to submit (or to submit narrowly)
- **Admissibility against the submitter** — anything said can be used in the SEC action and in
  **private litigation** (discoverable to plaintiffs) and shared with **DOJ**.
- **Previewing the defense** — you hand the staff your theory before any litigation.
- **Locking in positions / admissions** — a concession made to persuade can bind you later.
- **Parallel criminal exposure** — if a criminal investigation is live or likely, a civil-track
  submission can be **fatal**; coordinate with criminal counsel (and consider Fifth-Amendment
  implications for individuals) **first**.
- **Weak facts** — if the record is bad, a submission may only **emphasize** it.

**Output:** a clear **submit / don't-submit / submit-narrowly** recommendation with the reasoning
and the documented downside — for counsel to decide.

## Architecting the submission (if one is made)

Tailor the architecture to the matter; common pillars:

1. **Legal arguments** — the staff cannot establish a required **element**: no **scienter**
   (recklessness/intent) for a fraud claim; **materiality** not met (argued from the total mix);
   **statute of limitations** (note the SEC limitations developments — e.g., *Gabelli v. SEC* on
   when the clock starts for penalties, and *Kokesh*/statutory changes affecting disgorgement
   timing); **jurisdiction** (e.g., *Howey* — the instrument is not a security); **reliance/loss
   causation** where relevant.
2. **Factual corrections** — true, record-supported corrections of the staff's factual premises
   (every fact must be **accurate and supportable** — a false statement to the SEC is its own
   violation).
3. **Equitable / discretionary factors** — **cooperation**, prompt **remediation**, the **Seaboard
   factors** (see `remedies-cooperation-and-parallel.md`), harm/no-harm, the individual's role,
   and the public-interest case for declination or leniency.
4. **Remedies argument** — if charges are likely, argue for **narrower remedies** (no/limited
   penalty, no bar/officer-and-director bar, no admissions).

### Drafting discipline
- **Lead with the strongest, cleanest argument**; do not bury it.
- **Concede nothing unnecessary**; assume every word is read by a prosecutor and a plaintiff.
- **Tie every fact to the record** and **every legal point to real authority**.
- Keep the document **focused** — a Wells submission is persuasion, not an exhaustive brief; the
  staff has format/length expectations (verify in the Enforcement Manual).

## Timing & logistics

- The staff sets a **deadline** for the submission (extensions are commonly negotiable). Docket it.
- A submission may be **written**; the staff may also permit an **in-person Wells meeting** with
  the staff (and sometimes a meeting with senior officials). Decide whether a meeting helps or
  exposes the client.
- Coordinate the submission with any **settlement** overture and with criminal counsel.
