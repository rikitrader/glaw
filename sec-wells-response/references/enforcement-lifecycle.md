# The SEC Enforcement Lifecycle — MUI to Resolution

A Wells notice does not arrive out of nowhere; it sits near the **end** of a defined enforcement
process. Knowing where a matter sits — and what each stage signals — drives the Wells strategy.
This file maps the lifecycle from the first inquiry to final resolution.

## 1 — MUI (Matter Under Inquiry)

The Division of Enforcement opens a **Matter Under Inquiry** to evaluate whether a fuller
investigation is warranted. It is **preliminary and non-public**; the staff gathers information
informally (voluntary document requests, public records, referrals from exams/OCIE, tips via the
whistleblower program / Form TCR). At this stage the staff has **no subpoena power** under a formal
order yet.

## 2 — Formal Order of Investigation

If the matter warrants, the staff obtains a **Formal Order of Investigation** (the Commission has
delegated authority to the Director of Enforcement to issue formal orders). The formal order:

- Authorizes the staff to **issue subpoenas** for documents and **testimony** (under oath).
- Defines the **scope** of the investigation.
- Remains **non-public**; a recipient of a subpoena is generally entitled to **request a copy** of
  the formal order to understand the scope.

Investigative **testimony** is taken under oath; a witness has the right to **counsel** and may
**assert the Fifth Amendment** (for individuals). Form **1662** ("Supplemental Information for
Persons Requested to Supply Information Voluntarily or Directed to Supply Information Pursuant to a
Commission Subpoena") sets out the witness's rights and the routine uses of the information —
including that it may be shared with **criminal** and other authorities.

## 3 — The Wells notice

When the staff has developed a record and concluded it intends to recommend an enforcement action,
it typically (as a matter of practice, not an absolute legal entitlement) gives the prospective
respondent a **Wells notice** — notice of the **proposed charges** and an opportunity to make a
**Wells submission** before the staff finalizes its recommendation to the Commission. (See
`the-wells-process-and-submission.md`.) The Wells notice signals the matter is **near a charging
decision**.

## 4 — The Commission's decision

The staff presents its recommendation (and any Wells submission) to the **Commission**, which votes
whether to authorize an action. The Commission may:

- **Decline** / close the matter (a termination notice may issue).
- Authorize an **administrative proceeding** (before an SEC Administrative Law Judge / the
  Commission) — note the constitutional developments on the use of in-house ALJ forums for certain
  remedies (e.g., *Lucia v. SEC*; *SEC v. Jarkesy*) — verify the current state of the law on forum.
- Authorize a **civil action** in **federal district court**.
- Authorize a negotiated **settled** action filed simultaneously with the charge.

## 5 — Resolution: settlement or litigation

- **Settlement.** Most enforcement actions settle. SEC settlements historically proceed on a
  **"neither admit nor deny"** basis, though the Commission has required **admissions** in certain
  cases (egregious conduct, harm, criminal parallel). A settlement is documented in an **Offer of
  Settlement** and the Commission's order or a consent judgment.
- **Litigation.** If not settled, the matter is **litigated** (in the chosen forum), with the SEC
  bearing the burden by a **preponderance of the evidence** in most claims (fraud scienter and
  certain elements still must be proven, but not the criminal "beyond a reasonable doubt"
  standard).

## Parallel proceedings (screen continuously)

Throughout the lifecycle, screen for **parallel** tracks that change the calculus:

- **DOJ / criminal** — the SEC may make a **criminal referral**; statements in the SEC track
  (testimony, a Wells submission) can be used by prosecutors. This is the single most important
  reason to coordinate with criminal-defense counsel **before** speaking.
- **SROs (FINRA)** and **state regulators** — separate but coordinated.
- **Private securities litigation** — a parallel class action; a Wells submission may become
  **discoverable** to plaintiffs.

Route the criminal-exposure analysis to `/glaw-investigations` / `/glaw-federal-trial-counsel`.

## How this seat applies it

1. **Locate the matter** in the lifecycle (MUI / formal order / Wells / post-recommendation) and
   read what the stage and the Wells notice **signal**.
2. **Screen for parallel proceedings** (DOJ, FINRA, state, private) **before** any submission
   strategy — coordinate with criminal counsel where exposure exists.
3. Map the **likely forum and remedies** the staff will seek (see `remedies-cooperation-and-parallel.md`).
4. Feed all of this into the **make / don't-make** and **architecture** decisions in
   `the-wells-process-and-submission.md`.
