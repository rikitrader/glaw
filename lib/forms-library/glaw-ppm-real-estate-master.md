## FEDERAL INCOME TAX CONSEQUENCES

The following is a summary of certain federal income tax consequences to the Investors. A complete discussion is beyond the scope of this summary. The income tax consequences of an acquisition of an Interest are uncertain and complex and may not be the same for all taxpayers. The following summary is based on the Code, regulations (the "**Treasury Regulations**"), court decisions and published IRS rulings in effect on the date of this Memorandum.

**Classification for Purposes of Section 1031.** The Trust Agreement has been structured with the intent that an Investor will be treated as acquiring an undivided interest in real estate, as opposed to a security or interest in a partnership, joint venture, association or trust. No ruling will be requested from the IRS. On July 20, 2004, the IRS issued Revenue Ruling 2004-86, 2004-2 C.B. 191, which held that, assuming the other requirements of Section 1031 are satisfied, a taxpayer's exchange of real property for an interest in the DST satisfies the requirements of Section 1031, based on conclusions that (1) the DST is treated as an entity separate from its owners; (2) the DST is an "investment" trust and not a "business entity"; (3) the DST is a "grantor trust"; and (4) the holders of interests in the DST are treated as directly owning interests in real property held by the DST. Special Tax Counsel to the Trust has rendered a Tax Opinion that the acquisition of Interests by an Investor should be treated as a direct acquisition of the Property for purposes of Section 1031. The Tax Opinion addresses only one aspect of qualifying under Section 1031. Other issues not addressed include: whether a prospective Investor has properly identified the replacement property within the 45-day time period; whether the Relinquished Property qualified as being held for investment purposes or in a trade or business; whether a prospective Investor will fall within the deferred exchange safe harbor rules; whether a prospective Investor doing a reverse exchange meets the qualifications in IRS Revenue Procedure 2000-37; whether some portion of the Property is "personal property" as opposed to "real property"; and whether amounts paid with respect to certain costs and expenses of the Offering will be deemed to constitute other consideration received in the exchange.

**Property Identification for Section 1031 Exchanges.** [Three-property rule, 200% rule and 95% rule discussion. Property identification rules are complex; Investors must consult their own qualified intermediaries and tax advisors.]

**Receipt of Boot.** If, in a Section 1031 Exchange, money is received or deemed received in addition to the like-kind property (referred to as "boot"), then gain on the Relinquished Property is recognized up to the amount of boot. The IRS may take the position that some amounts paid into the Reserve Accounts and amounts paid in connection with the Offering constitute boot. Special Tax Counsel is not opining as to whether any such amounts will be considered an acquisition of real estate or boot.

**Excess Business Losses May Not Be Currently Deductible.** For tax years beginning after December 31, 2020, excess business losses of a taxpayer other than a corporation are not allowed for the taxable year and are carried forward as part of the taxpayer's net operating loss carryforward. The threshold amount for 2024 is $305,000 (or twice that for a joint return), indexed for inflation.

**Limit on Business Interest Deductions.** The Business Interest Limitation in Code Section 163(j) limits certain taxpayers' annual deductions for "business interest" to the sum of the 30% ATI Limit plus certain business interest income. Because the Trust is classified as a fixed investment trust and a grantor trust, the Business Interest Limitation applies, if at all, to Investors and not to the Trust. Certain Investors that qualify as "small businesses" (average annual gross receipts not exceeding $25 million) are excepted (the "**Small Business Exception**"). If an Investor makes a Real Property Business Election, the Investor must use the alternative depreciation system ("**ADS**") and will be ineligible for Bonus Depreciation under Code Section 168(k). [Insert full discussion of trade-or-business determination under Code Section 162 and 199A interaction.]

**Deduction for Qualified Business Income.** Code Section 199A generally provides that a noncorporate taxpayer can deduct 20% of "qualified business income." There is substantial uncertainty as to whether a taxpayer's ownership of real estate subject to a triple-net lease can qualify as a "trade or business" for purposes of Code Section 199A. IRS Revenue Procedure 2019-38 created a safe harbor but specifically excludes triple net leases. The application of Code Section 199A will differ based on each Investor's facts and circumstances.

**Tax Deficiency, Penalties and Interest.** If an IRS audit disqualifies an Investor's proposed Section 1031 Exchange, the Investor will be taxed on its gain on the sale of the Relinquished Property, and the IRS will assess interest and could assess penalties. The Code provides for accuracy-related penalties equal to 20% of the portion of the underpayment to which the penalty applies. The Tax Opinion is not binding on the IRS and does not provide a guarantee against an adverse tax result.

**Taxable Income.** It is expected that an Investor's Interests will generate annual taxable income in excess of the cash distributable to such Investor. If an Investor has a low tax basis in the Relinquished Property, such Investor will have a low tax basis in his, her or its Interests, and depreciation deductions will be less.

**Net Income and Loss of Each Investor.** Each Investor will be required to determine his, her or its own net income or loss from the Property for income tax purposes and to keep separate records. The 3.8% Medicare Contributions Tax may apply to certain U.S. individuals, estates, or trusts.

**Taxation of Tax-Exempt Investors.** Tax-exempt entities are subject to taxation on their unrelated business taxable income ("**UBTI**"). Because the Interests are treated as direct interests in the Property, tax-exempt investors will be deemed to be carrying on the activities of the Trust. To the extent the acquisition or improvement of the real estate is financed with "acquisition indebtedness," a proportionate share of rent and capital gain is treated as UBTI. Because the Property is debt-financed, a portion of certain tax-exempt investors' rental income and gains will generate UBTI. **Tax Impact of Sale of the Property.** If the Property is sold or otherwise disposed of (such as by foreclosure), the Investors will likely recognize taxable income. The amount realized will include the amount of any debt assumed or eliminated. The 3.8% Medicare Contribution Tax is likely to apply to any net gain.

**State and Local Laws.** Some states adopt Section 1031 in whole, others in part, and still others impose their own requirements. Each prospective Investor must consult his, her, or its own tax advisor regarding state and local tax consequences.

## ERISA CONSIDERATIONS

The following is a summary of certain considerations associated with an investment in the Trust by Benefit Plan Investors. Each Benefit Plan Investor considering investing the assets of an IRA, or a pension, profit sharing, 401(k), Keogh or other employee benefit plan should satisfy itself that such investment is consistent with its fiduciary obligations under ERISA and other applicable law, is made in accordance with the documents governing the plan or IRA, and satisfies the prudence and diversification requirements of Sections 404(a)(1)(B) and 404(a)(1)(C) of ERISA. Each Benefit Plan Investor should also determine that an investment in the Trust will not impair the liquidity of the plan or IRA and that, even though the Interests are expected to produce UBTI, the purchase and holding of an Interest is still consistent with the fiduciary obligations of the Benefit Plan Investor.

**Treatment of the Trust under ERISA.** ERISA and the Code do not define "plan assets." The DOL has issued the Plan Asset Rules, which provide that the underlying assets of an entity in which a plan purchases an "equity interest" will be deemed to be assets of the investing plan unless certain exceptions apply. Interests in the Trust should be treated as "equity interests." One exception to the look-through rule provides that an investing plan's assets will not include any of the underlying assets of an entity if at all times less than 25% of each class of "equity" interests are held by Benefit Plan Investors. The Sponsor and the Administrative Trustee intend to take such steps as may be necessary to limit ownership by Benefit Plan Investors to less than 25%, and thereby qualify for the 25% exemption. Each Benefit Plan Investor should consult with its counsel with respect to the potential applicability of ERISA and Code Section 4975 to such investment.
