============================ CAUSE-SPECIFIC GOTCHAS — VERIFY BEFORE FILING ============================

ADA TITLE II / TITLE III — KEY TRAPS:

1. NO INDIVIDUAL LIABILITY. Titles II and III run against public ENTITIES and persons who OWN/OPERATE
   public accommodations — NOT individual employees/officials in their individual capacity. Drop individual
   defendants unless a circuit-specific theory or § 1983 (Title II) supports them. (Most circuits bar.)

2. TITLE III = INJUNCTIVE RELIEF ONLY. Private plaintiffs CANNOT recover damages under Title III
   (42 U.S.C. § 12188(a) borrows 42 U.S.C. § 2000a-3(a) — equitable only). Damages are available ONLY to
   the U.S. Attorney General. Do NOT plead a Title III damages count for a private plaintiff.

3. TITLE II DAMAGES REQUIRE INTENT. Compensatory damages under Title II/§ 504 require INTENTIONAL
   DISCRIMINATION; most circuits apply the "DELIBERATE INDIFFERENCE" standard (actual knowledge + failure
   to act by an official with authority to correct). A few circuits require discriminatory animus. CHECK YOUR
   CIRCUIT. No punitive damages against public entities (Barnes v. Gorman). No damages for negligence alone.

4. STANDING (INJUNCTIVE). For prospective injunctive relief, plaintiff must show a real and immediate threat
   of FUTURE injury — plead intent to return / deterrence / proximity (Lujan; tester-standing varies by circuit;
   watch Acheson Hotels v. Laufer mootness fallout). Past harm alone does NOT confer standing for an injunction.

5. SOVEREIGN IMMUNITY / ABROGATION. Title II validly abrogates state Eleventh Amendment immunity ONLY where
   the conduct also violates the Fourteenth Amendment (US v. Georgia; Tennessee v. Lane — fundamental-right
   access to courts). For "non-fundamental" public services, abrogation is uncertain — plead the constitutional
   hook or expect an immunity defense. § 504 abrogation rests on Spending-Clause waiver (federal funding).

6. EXHAUSTION. ADA Titles II and III generally do NOT require administrative exhaustion before suit
   (unlike Title I employment / EEOC). BUT if also pleading Rehab Act § 504 in some contexts (e.g., IDEA overlap),
   exhaustion may apply (Fry v. Napoleon). Verify.

7. STATUTE OF LIMITATIONS. ADA Title II/III borrow the forum STATE'S personal-injury limitations period
   (varies 1–6 yrs). Confirm the applicable state period and any continuing-violation theory.

8. PRE-SUIT NOTICE. Federal ADA has NO pre-suit notice requirement, BUT several STATE analog statutes and a
   few states (and some local rules) impose pre-suit notice/cure periods for accessibility suits — check state law
   if pendent claims are pled.

9. STANDARDS. Architectural-barrier claims should reference the 2010 ADA Standards for Accessible Design
   (28 C.F.R. Pt. 36 App.) and, for Title II facilities, 28 C.F.R. § 35.151. "Readily achievable" (existing
   facilities) vs. strict compliance (new construction/alterations) is fact-specific.

10. JURY. Jury attaches to LEGAL claims (Title II / § 504 damages). Claims for SOLELY equitable relief
    (Title III injunction) carry NO Seventh Amendment jury right — omit the jury demand if no damages count.

11. FEES. Prevailing-plaintiff fees under 42 U.S.C. § 12205 are discretionary but routine; a prevailing
    DEFENDANT recovers fees only if the suit was frivolous (Christiansburg). Expert fees recoverable under § 12205.
